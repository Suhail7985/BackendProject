India and Other Tropical Nations Must Brace for Record-Breaking Summer Heat and Humidity Soon: Study

As India sizzles over before reaching its core summer month, even wearing a shirt has become a topic of debate. On one hand, decency is supposedly important in our current society; on the other, as mentioned earlier, it’s sizzling freakin’ hot!

El Niño, a weather phenomenon characterised by warmer-than-average sea surface temperatures in the equatorial Pacific Ocean, influences weather patterns worldwide. During El Niño events, heat and moisture are pumped into the upper atmosphere, leading to increased temperatures and humidity levels in tropical regions like India. The study found that these upper-level temperatures are at their hottest typically five months after the El Niño peak.

As most must be aware by now, the most recent peak of a Super El Niño occurred in December 2023. While we’re already enduring an unusually parched winter and summer due to its influence, the study explains that the phenomenon’s true nature is expected to be felt during the upcoming summer months. The researchers predict average maximum wet bulb temperatures on land to rise to 26.2°C, with a 68% chance of breaking existing records.

The wet bulb temperature is a measure of heat and humidity that accounts for the cooling effect of evaporation. The metric serves as a better way of ascertaining the discomfort that humans feel under the combination of heat and humidity (since high humidity can reduce the cooling potential of our bodies) and is crucial for assessing the risk of heat-related illnesses and heat stress. In warm-humid environments like the tropics, wet bulb temperatures above 30°C can lead to dangerous levels of heat stress, particularly for vulnerable populations.

The study found that areas like northern India, which frequently experience humid heat stress, are particularly at risk. The study suggests a 50% chance of record-breaking heat and humidity in northern India this summer. Other regions, such as the Sahel region in Africa, also face elevated risks, with a 35% chance of record humid heat.
